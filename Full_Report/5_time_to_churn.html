E) Time to churn

Research Question: Which customer segments and engagement levers (coupons, campaigns, spend intensity) meaningfully extend household survival, so we know whom to target and which promotional tactics actually drive profitable retention?

For this, we investigate:

  • Time until churn survival analysis (Customer survival")
  • Understanding how factors affect churn timing
  • Campaigns and coupon tactics as concrete explanatory variables (marketing actions)

Learning references

We went through the Complete Journey data dictionary plus the Lecture 10 slides. Lecture 10 explains Kaplan Meier estimators, the log rank test, and the Cox proportional hazards model so we refer to those ideas whenever we interpret retention patterns.

Data preparation approach

  • build per household purchase history with spend, discount share, product breadth, and trip frequency features
  • set a four week buffer before the end of the observation window to avoid labeling late shoppers as churned without evidence
  • merge coupon redemptions and campaign assignments so we can compare engagement strategies
  • keep the demographic classes that were also used inside the customer value map folder which lets us cross reference the same segments across workstreams

Cox proportional hazards model

We move to the multivariate Cox model as we want to control for other variables (spending) in addition to age, to check whether coupon and campaign levers remain predictive after controlling for both of them (spending and demographics).

The underlying Research Question for this section is After controlling for core behavioral metrics (trips, spend) and demographics, do campaign touches and coupon engagement still reduce churn hazard, and which covariates remain significant predictors of customer survival?

Takeaway Analysis:

Higher overall spend and more trips per household reduce hazard substantially, and the Cox summary above shows why: log_trips carries a hazard ratio of 0.57 with a p-value very smal, while log_total_spend sits at 0.67 with a small p value. Those < 1 hazard ratios mean that even after controlling for campaigns and coupons, households that shop more often or spend more decline in risk at each point in time. Among the demographic controls, only demo1_Age Group2 is significant (HR 0.58, p 0.04), signalling that Age Group2 households stay longer relative to the Age Group1 baseline. The Age Group6 dummy trends higher (HR 1.13) but its p-value 0.70 shows that the excess hazard is not statistically distinguishable from the base cohort. The concordance of roughly 0.81 indicates reasonable discriminatory power, as it is above 0.5 (random guessing). It means the Cox model correctly orders the survival risk for roughly 81% of all comparable household pairs. In other words: when the model predicts that household A should churn before household B, that ordering matches the observed outcomes about 4/5 of the time - a solid level of discriminatory power.

Final answer and next steps

We set out to learn whether campaigns and coupons truly extend survival or simply correlate with healthy shoppers. The KaplanMeier views show a clear descriptive lift: coupon redeemers and campaign-reached households stay active roughly 1530 percentage points longer by week 90, and the demographic log-rank contrasts highlight the at-risk Age Group6 segment. Yet once the Cox proportional hazards model controls for trips, spend, and age, campaign and coupon indicators lose statistical significance while log_trips and log_total_spend remain strongly protective (hazard ratios < 0.7). That means the tactics only pay off when they trigger incremental engagement rather than just targeting customers who were already loyal.

Next steps:

  • Design experiments that push low-engagement shoppersespecially Age Group6toward their first coupon redemption or an extra trip, then re-run the Cox model on uplifted cohorts.
  • Refresh the survival dataset monthly to monitor whether new campaigns actually shift the behavioral drivers (trips/spend) that reduce hazard.
  • Enrich the model with recency or additional digital-touch metrics so we can detect campaign formats that change shopping cadence earlier.

ANNEX

Covariate reference
  • coupon_user_flag: Indicator that the household redeemed at least one coupon.
  • campaign_reached_flag: Indicator that the shopper appeared in the campaign table (touched by >=1 campaign).
  • log_unique_campaigns: Log-transformed count of distinct campaigns received; keeps very high exposure from dominating the fit.
  • coupons_redeemed: Raw count of redeemed coupons; complements the binary flag above.
  • trip_frequency_per_week: Trips divided by observed weeks, capturing cadence independent of tenure length.
  • avg_trip_value: Average basket value (total spend / trips), so a positive coefficient means larger tickets accelerate churn.
  • log_total_spend: Log of cumulative spend during the observation window. The hazard ratio around 0.67 shows that heavier spenders churn more slowly.
  • coupon_share: Share of spend funded by coupons (loyalty_discount_share); gauges dependence on discounts.
  • log_trips: Log of number of trips. With a hazard ratio 0.57 it confirms that more trips materially lower churn risk.
  • demo1_Age Group2 demo1_Age Group6: Dummy variables from pd.get_dummies(demo_class_1, prefix='demo1', drop_first=True). demo_class_1 is the age-based segmentation in the demographic table. Age Group1 is the omitted reference, so each dummys hazard ratio compares that segment against Age Group1 after controlling for the other covariates.

demo_class_1 holds six ordered age clusters supplied by the retailer (Age Group1 through Age Group6). Because the names are abstract rather than explicit ages, we interpret them as relative cohortse.g., the significant demo1_Age Group2 coefficient above means that the Age Group2 households show about a 42% lower hazard than the Age Group1 baseline, all else equal.
